## Nutritionist's Base Plan Summary

Your nutritionist uses a **plate method** (not calorie counting):
- **1/2 plate** vegetables
- **1/4 plate** protein (200g raw chicken/beef/ground beef, or 1 can tuna + 50g cheese)
- **1/4 plate** carbs (50g raw rice, or 200g potato, or 60g raw pasta, or 100g legumes + 50g cooked rice)
- **1 tsp olive oil** (or substitute: handful of nuts or 1/2 avocado)
- Gym days: 5 eating occasions (breakfast, optional snack, lunch, pre-training snack, post-training dinner)
- Rest days: 5 eating occasions (quick breakfast, lunch, snack, optional snack, dinner)

This plan was designed for **fat loss with strength training only**. The adjustments below layer in hybrid athlete needs.

### Deficit Severity Note

This is an aggressive deficit (~33% below estimated TDEE) for a hybrid athlete training 6 days/week. It will produce rapid fat loss but carries risks that must be monitored:
- Performance decline in lifts or runs → increase training-day calories by 100–200
- Excessive fatigue, poor sleep, or mood changes → increase daily average by 100–150
- Lifts stalling for 2+ consecutive weeks → increase heavy-lift-day calories
- Weight dropping faster than 1 kg/week consistently → calories may be too low

Protein at 120g/day (1.26 g/kg) is on the lower end for athletes in a deficit. Prioritize hitting this minimum every day — do not go below.

## Macro Rationale

### Protein — 120g/day (1.26 g/kg), constant every day
- Nutritionist-prescribed target. The plate method with 200g raw meat at lunch+dinner naturally delivers ~90g from those two meals. Breakfast and snacks cover the remaining ~30g.
- Distribute across 4 feedings: ~30g per meal on average.
- Whey protein powder is used as a recipe ingredient (10–15g in pancakes, licuados) per the nutritionist's plan — not required as a standalone supplement at every meal.
- Prioritize: chicken breast, beef, tuna, eggs, yogurt, cottage cheese.

### Carbohydrates — periodized by day type (175–275g)
- The primary energy lever. Adequate for training performance at this calorie level.
- Highest on heavy lift days (275g) — use 75g raw rice instead of standard 50g at one meal.
- Standard nutritionist portions (50g raw rice / 200g potato / 60g pasta) on other days.
- Lowest on rest days (175g) — standard portions, no extras.

### Fat — 58–69g (0.6–0.7 g/kg)
- Adequate for hormonal health and satiety.
- Nutritionist uses 5ml (1 tsp) olive oil per meal, with peanut butter and nuts as fat sources in breakfast/snacks.
- Higher on rest days to improve satiety when carbs are lower.
- Sources: olive oil, peanut butter, nuts, eggs, naturally occurring fat in meat.

### Fiber — 30–40g/day
- The plate method's 1/2 plate of vegetables is excellent for this.

## Recomposition Strategy

### Core Principles
1. **Calorie cycling** drives the recomp: surplus on demanding days, deficit on easy/rest days.
2. **Protein is constant** — never compromised regardless of calorie target.
3. **Carbs are the lever** — adjusted up/down to control total calories while fueling performance.
4. **Fat stays in a healthy range** — slightly higher on low-carb days for satiety and hormones.
5. **Weekly average matters more than any single day** — one bad day doesn't derail recomp if the weekly average holds.
6. **Plate method for simplicity** — use your nutritionist's plate method as the foundation and adjust portion sizes by day type rather than weighing every gram.

### What to Track
- Weekly average weight (weigh daily, use 7-day rolling average to smooth fluctuations)
- Lifting performance (are weights/reps progressing?)
- Running performance (are times/distances improving?)
- Visual progress (photos every 2–4 weeks)
- Energy levels and recovery quality

### When to Adjust
- **Weight stalls for 2+ weeks AND lifts aren't progressing:** reduce rest-day calories by 100–150
- **Performance declining or excessive fatigue:** increase training-day carbs by 25–50g
- **Weight dropping faster than 0.5 kg/week:** increase daily average by 100–150 kcal (add to rest or run days)
- **Weight increasing faster than expected:** reduce heavy-lift-day surplus by 100–150 kcal

## Phase-Specific Notes

### Acclimatization (Weeks 1–3) — CURRENT
- Establish baseline eating patterns and adherence
- Focus on hitting protein targets consistently before worrying about exact carb/fat splits
- Begin increasing water intake to target
- Log food for at least 5 days this week to calibrate portion awareness
- Get a body fat estimate if possible (navy method, calipers, or DEXA)

### Extension (Weeks 4–6)
- Apply deload nutrition in week 4
- Review 3 weeks of data; adjust calories if needed
- By now, meal prep routine should be established

### Deload — Week 7
- Full deload: reduce all calorie targets by 10%
- Focus on recovery foods: anti-inflammatory, nutrient-dense
- Good week to do bloodwork if desired

### Progression (Weeks 8–9)
- Running volume increases (longer intervals, 8–12 min blocks)
- May need to increase run-day carbs by 20–30g
- Reassess TDEE — running is now burning more calories

### Consolidation (Weeks 10–11)
- Continuous running begins (no walk breaks)
- Ensure glycogen is topped off before Saturday long runs
- Consider a carb-focused snack Friday evening (pre-long-run loading)

### Graduation (Week 12)
- Lifting deload + longest continuous runs
- Maintenance calories; assess 12-week results
- Plan next phase based on progress
